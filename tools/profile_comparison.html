Both curves are normalised so their mean speed over one full cycle equals 1 — since both profiles run the same total sweep distance in the same Sweep time, this makes them directly comparable. Speed is signed: positive while sweeping back→forward, negative on the return leg. Sine follows a cosine taper (speed never drops below 20% of centre speed); Reciprocal follows an exponential-decay taper (down to 10%) — so Reciprocal slows more aggressively near the wafer edge and runs faster through the centre to compensate.

Endpoint ramp (Accel/Jerk): the firmware no longer uses a fixed-angle ramp. Instead it estimates each leg's peak speed (a no-ramp pass), then sizes the ramp so the arm can decelerate to a stop at Accel (°/s²) before the reversal — rampDeg = vPeak² / (2×accel), up to 3× slower at the very edge. Jerk (°/s³) controls the ramp's shape: low jerk blends toward a smoother quintic "smootherstep" (gentler onset/exit), high jerk toward a sharper cubic smoothstep. Because Reciprocal's peak speed is higher than Sine's for the same settings, it gets a wider ramp for the same Accel — see the computed ramp widths below the charts. This applies to both profiles at every direction reversal, independent of the speed-profile shape — for Edge↔(•) this means the arm decelerates as it reaches the wafer centre, even though the profile shape alone would have it at its fastest there.

Why the flat plateau near each edge? The cosine/reciprocal tapers are clamped at their 20%/10% floors once the distance from centre exceeds ~87%/90% of the half-sweep — so speed is genuinely constant (flat) across that outer band of the sweep, independent of the ramp. The endpoint ramp then adds one extra dip right at the very reversal point, inside that flat band.

Acceleration (bottom chart) is the numerical derivative of the signed speed curve. The big spikes at each reversal come from the endpoint ramp's slow-down/speed-up and from the model's instantaneous direction flip — this dwell-time model doesn't simulate decelerating through zero, so speed jumps directly from a small positive value to a small negative one (or vice versa) at each reversal.
